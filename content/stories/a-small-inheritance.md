When my aunt died, she left me nothing but a tin box and instructions not to open it until I had learned to be still.

I was twenty-six and had never been still in my life. I was the kind of person who checked their telephone at red lights and ate lunch while answering correspondence. The word *still* made me feel mildly ill, the way the word *beige* does to people who love colour.

The tin box sat on my bookshelf for three years. It was small — about the size of a thick paperback — and painted dark green, with a tiny clasp that had no lock. I could have opened it at any moment. But the instructions had been handwritten by my aunt on lavender paper, and she had underlined the word *still* twice, which with her meant she was serious.

My aunt had been a woman of significant silences. She had lived alone since her husband died in 1987, in a house full of books and clocks and a single very old cat named Possibly. She had been the only person in the family who never offered unsolicited opinions. When she did speak, people leaned forward.

I began, in my thirty-first year, to sit quietly in the mornings before work. Not meditation exactly — I was too impatient for meditation — just sitting. Ten minutes at first. Then twenty. I would watch the light move across the kitchen wall and try not to think of anything in particular.

It took eight months before I realized I had become, in my own approximate way, still.

I opened the tin box on a Tuesday in November.

Inside was a brass compass, a folded note, and a photograph of my aunt at perhaps my age, standing on a hillside somewhere green and windy, laughing at whoever held the camera. She looked entirely like herself.

The note said: *The compass doesn't point north. It points toward wherever you most need to go. I've never been able to tell if that's a blessing or a curse. Perhaps you'll have better luck. With all my love, R.*

I held the compass in my palm. The needle swung slowly, settled, and pointed — as far as I could tell — directly at the bookshelf.

I laughed for the first time in what felt like weeks.

"All right," I said aloud, to no one. "All right."
